Visualizing the System of Hebrew Accents

This page is designed to provide a visual way to understand the rules of Biblical Hebrew cantillation, a system of markings called accents that provide grammatical information (similar to English punctuation, but much more detailed). This is based on The Syntax of Masoretic Accents by James D. Price, except here Kadma V'Azla is treated as one accent instead of two. Most of this does not apply to books of Psalms, Proverbs, and Job, which use a different system briefly sketched in the last section.

How to use this page: To find out what accent can come after a given accent, follow these rules for moving from cell to cell in the first table below:

  • Within a row, you can only move directly left, although you may optionally skip any conjunctive accents (those in parentheses).
  • Once you reach the end of the row, you may then jump to any cell in that column or a column to the right.
  • If an accent appears by itself – i.e. with nothing from its row directly before it – then it may need to be substituted (see the second table below).
  • An asterisk (*) means this accent can only be used once per verse, as the first of its rank or higher before Sof-pasuk (these are Etnachta and Segol).

Colors: When hovering over the tables below:

  • Light red indicate all accents which could have come before the current accent (without jumping),
  • Orange indicates the current accent or one its substitutes,
  • Yellow indicates all accents which can come immediately after the current accent, and
  • Light green indicates all accents which could come further after the current accent (without jumping).

Note: Conjunctive accents (those in parentheses) have less clear-cut rules defining their usage. For example, many of them do not have a just one accent that they always pair with – duplicates in the table below are marked in light gray. Additionally, more than one such accent can appear in a row. Aside from the two cases listed in the “Less common substitutions table” where a regular accent is substituted for a conjunctive one, this additional accent can be a repeat of what comes after it, a Munach, a Mapach, a Kadma, or a Darga (from most to least common).

Understanding the System of Hebrew Accents

Analogy: In a long verse, the accents at the ends of their respective rows have the following approximate correspondences in English punctuation:

  • The Emperors Sof-pasuk and Etnachta correspond to a period and a semicolon or dash, respectively.
  • The Kings Segol and Zakef correspond to dashes or commas.
  • The Duke R'via corresponds to a comma, a colon, or an unmarked boundary between two phrases.
  • The Count Pazer corresponds to a colon or an unmarked boundary between two phrases.
  • The conjuctive accents (those in parentheses) are used to mark words within a phrase.
In shorter verses, this correspondence often shifts up, e.g. with Etnachta corresponding to a comma or even an unmarked boundary in an extremely short verse.

Example 2: The classic example of accents changing the apparent meaning of the text is Isaiah 40:3. The King James Version translates this line as: “The voice of him that crieth in the wilderness, Prepare ye the way of the LORD, make straight in the desert a highway for our God.” In particular, “in the wilderness” is grouped together with the phrase about a voice crying out. Most modern translations instead look something like the following:

ק֣וֹל קוֹרֵ֔א
בַּמִּדְבָּ֕ר
פַּנּ֖וּ דֶּ֣רֶךְ יְיָ֑
יַשְּׁרוּ֙ בָּעֲרָבָ֔ה
מְסִלָּ֖ה לֵאלֹהֵֽינוּ׃
A voice calls out,
“In the wilderness,
clear away a road for Adonai!
Level out in the desert,
a highway for our God!”

“In the wilderness” has the accent T'lisha-g'dolah, an accent which does not go together with anything else by definition. This suggests that “in the wilderness” should at least be separated with a comma from what comes before it. Although it could in theory still be in reference to the voice (just separated for poetic or rhetorical effect), the strong parallelism of road and desert imagery of the final four phrases of this verse make that seem unlikely to me. The indentation is added to make the parallelism easier to spot.

Accents in the Three Poetic Books

The poetic books of Psalms, Proverbs, and Job use a distinct system of accents. Although some of the same symbols are used, they mostly function in different ways. (Thus, a different font is used to keep the two systems visually distinct.) A different system is necessary because of the unique structure of Hebrew poetry. For example, verses of Hebrew poetry are often made up of three parallel verselets. Ole V'Yored was therefore added as a parallel accent to Etnachta – together, the two can divide a verse into three equal parts.

In general, this system has many more exceptions and special cases than the above. For example, Mapach-l'garmeh can be inserted before nearly every other accent, transcending its place in the hierarchy. Some of these accents also have conjunctive forms, and there are other conjunctive accents that exist which are not listed here.

An asterisk (*) means this accent can only be used once per verse. A dagger (†) means this accent is frequently used in ways beyond what this table suggests.
