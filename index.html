Visualizing the System of Hebrew Accents

The Hebrew alphabet, like the Arabic alphabet, only contains letters representing consonants – so for thousands of year the texts of the Tanakh (the Hebrew Bible) contained no markings for vowels. In most cases, a word’s context is enough for a Hebrew speaker to intuitively know how to pronounce the word – just like how English speakers intuitively know to pronounce “tear” differently in “I shed a tear” as opposed to “I tear some paper.”

But after the destruction of the second temple, fewer and fewer Jews spoke Hebrew as their native language – mostly interacting with these texts through oral recitation. Instead of each word’s pronunciation being obvious, the Hebrew text perhaps began to look to its readers something like the English translation below does to you:

והיו למאורת ברקיע השמים להאיר על הארץ ויהי כן
N THY WLL SRVE AS LGTS ON TH SRFCE OF TH SKY IN ORDR TO SHN ON TH ERTH N IT WS SO

A group of sages called the Masoretes began to add diacritics to the text to aid with recitation. Most well known are the markings they added to represent vowels, called Nikud. With just Nikud, the intended pronunciation of the text can be determined just by looking at it:

וְהָיוּ לִמְאוֹרֹת בִּרְקִיעַ הַשָּׁמַיִם לְהָאִיר עַל־הָאָרֶץ וַיְהִי־כֵן
and they will serve as lights on the surface of the sky in order to shine on the earth and it was so

But, the Masoretes added other markings, called Ta’amim, which indicated more than just pronunciation. After thousands of years of regular recitation, Jews had developed a tradition of chanting the text in a way that mirrored its grammatical structure. These Ta’amim, called cantillation marks or accents in English, described how to chant the text, and thus, also described its grammatical structure. Like with punctuation marks in English, the intended structure of the text can now be understood just by looking at it:

וְהָי֤וּ לִמְאוֹרֹת֙ בִּרְקִ֣יעַ הַשָּׁמַ֔יִם לְהָאִ֖יר עַל־הָאָ֑רֶץ וַֽיְהִי־כֵֽן׃
And they will serve as lights on the surface of the sky, in order to shine on the earth – and it was so.

Overview

I like to think about accents as grouping the words in a verse into phrases (each of which is a group of usually one or two words that are closely related) and in turn grouping these phrases into clauses (each of which is usually a sentence fragment with a subject and object) – the way accents define these divisions will be outlined later.

First, as an example, here is the verse from the introduction, Genesis 1:15, divided into phrases and clauses based on its accents – hover to see the correspondences between the Hebrew and the English (the colors are described in the next section):

וְהָי֤וּ לִמְאוֹרֹת֙ בִּרְקִ֣יעַ הַשָּׁמַ֔יִם לְהָאִ֖יר עַל־הָאָ֑רֶץ וַֽיְהִי־כֵֽן׃
And they will serve as lights on the surface of the sky, in order to shine on the earth – and it was so.

As you may have noticed above, it is the accent on the last word of each clause that actually corresponds to an English punctuation mark. These accent marks are called remote disjunctive accents, and here is an overview of the four most common of them and their approximate relationship to English punctuation:

סוף־פסֽוק׃ Sof-pasuk These large double-dots that look like a English colon (which are always accompanied by a small line below one of the letters) mark the end of a verse – it corresponds to a period at the end of a verse
אתנחת֑א Etnachta This wishbone-shaped accent below a letter splits a verse into its two largest parts – it corresponds to a semicolon in long verses, a dash in medium-length verses, and a comma in short verses
זק֔ף Zakef These small double-dots above a letter mark the most common division within one of a verse’s parts – it usually corresponds to a comma, or nothing in short verses
רב֗יע R’via This single dot above a letter marks a lesser division within one of a verse’s parts – it often corresponds to a colon, or to a comma in long verses and nothing in shorter verses

(Warning: The remainder of this article now switches tones from explanatory to technical, and assumes enough familiarity with Hebrew to easily pick out accents.)

The way a verse is divide into phrases and clauses is entirely determined by the type of an accent:

  • Conjunctive accents indicate that the word they’re on is part of a phrase that includes the following word
  • Disjunctive accents indicate that the word they’re on is the end of a phrase, which can be either:
    • Near, indicating that the word they’re on is part of a clause that includes the following phrase
    • Remote, indicating that the word they’re on is the end of a clause

All of the possible accents, as well as their types, are explained in the next section.

Visualization of the System

This section provides a complete description of the system of accents used in all the books of the Tanakh except Psalms, Proverbs, and Job (see the last section). The heart of this section is the first table below. In the language of the overview above, each row of this table is a possible clause (read right-to-left), and each pair of accents with one parenthesized and the other not is a possible phrase within that clause. A clause may start anywhere in its a row, but no disjunctive accents between its start and end can be skipped. Once you reach a remote disjunctive accent, i.e. the end of a row, you can then jump to somewhere else in the table. Listed below are the complete rules for moving from cell-to-cell in this table.

Although this way to describing the system in a single table appears to be a new contribution (if you know of any source that has a similar table, please let me know!), the source used for all of the information in this section and the next is The Syntax of Masoretic Accents by James D. Price – except here Kadma V’Azla is treated as one accent instead of two.

Rules for the first table below: To find out what accent(s) can come after a given accent, follow these rules for moving from cell-to-cell:

  • Within a row, you can only move directly left, although you may optionally skip any conjunctive accents (those in parentheses).
  • Once you reach the end of the row, you may then jump to any cell in that column or a column to the right.
  • If an accent appears by itself – i.e. with nothing from its row directly before it – then it may need to be substituted (see the second table below).
  • An asterisk (*) means this accent can only be used once per verse, as the first of its rank or higher in the verse (these are Etnachta and Segol).

Colors: When hovering over the tables below:

  • Light red indicate all accents which could have come before the current accent (without jumping),
  • Orange indicates the current accent or one its substitutes,
  • Yellow indicates all accents which can come immediately after the current accent, and
  • Light green indicates all accents which could come further after the current accent (without jumping).

Note: Conjunctive accents (those in parentheses) have less clear-cut rules defining their usage. For example, many of them do not have a just one accent that they always pair with – duplicates in the table below are marked in light gray. Additionally, more than one such accent can appear in a row. Aside from the two cases where a disjunctive accent is substituted for a conjunctive one listed in the “Less common substitutions table,” this additional accent can be a repeat of what comes after it, a Munach, a Mapach, a Kadma, or a Darga (from most to least common).

Placement of accents: Most accents are placed on the accented syllable of the word, but some are always placed on the left-most letter of the word – specifically Pashta, Zarka, Segol, and T’lisha. Only two rare accents are always placed on the right-most letter of the word – specifically Y’tiv and T’lisha-g’dolah. For these latter two categories, modern texts will often duplicate the accent on the stressed syllable, to help with proper pronunciation and chanting.

In the year of the six-hundredth year of Noah’s life,
in the second month,
on the seventeenth day of the month –

Note that the accent on the first word of the first line and second-to-last line above is a Kadma (the conjunctive substitute for Kadma V’Azla), but the accent on the first word of the second line is a Pashta. The accent in the middle of the first word of the second line is a duplicate Pashta, as described above.

Example 2: The classic example of accents changing the apparent meaning of the text is Isaiah 40:3. The King James Version translates this line as: “The voice of him that crieth in the wilderness, Prepare ye the way of the Lord, make straight in the desert a highway for our God.” In particular, “in the wilderness” is grouped together with the phrase about a voice crying out. Most modern translations instead look something like the following:

ק֣וֹל קוֹרֵ֔א
בַּמִּדְבָּ֕ר
פַּנּ֖וּ דֶּ֣רֶךְ יְיָ֑
יַשְּׁרוּ֙ בָּעֲרָבָ֔ה
מְסִלָּ֖ה לֵאלֹהֵֽינוּ׃
A voice calls out,
“In the wilderness,
clear away a road for Adonai!
Level out in the desert,
a highway for our God!”

“In the wilderness” has the accent Zakef-gadol, an accent which does not go together with anything else by definition. This suggests that “in the wilderness” should at least be separated with a comma from what comes before it. Although it could in theory still be in reference to the voice (just separated for poetic or rhetorical effect), the strong parallelism of road and desert imagery of the final four phrases of this verse makes that seem unlikely to me. The indentation is added to make the parallelism easier to spot.

Accents in the Three Poetic Books

The poetic books of Psalms, Proverbs, and Job use a distinct system of accents. Although some of the same symbols are used, they mostly function in different ways. A different system is necessary because of the unique structure of Hebrew poetry. For example, verses of Hebrew poetry are often made up of three parallel verselets. Ole V’Yored was therefore added as a parallel accent to Etnachta – together, the two can divide a verse into three equal parts.

In general, this system has many more exceptions and special cases than the above. For example, Mapach-l’garmeh can be inserted before nearly every other accent, transcending its place in the hierarchy. Some of these accents also have conjunctive forms, and there are other conjunctive accents that exist which are not listed here.
