Visualizing the System of Hebrew Accents

This page is designed to provide a visual way to understand the rules of Biblical Hebrew cantillation, a system of markings called accents that provide grammatical information (similar to English punctuation, but much more detailed). This is based on The Syntax of Masoretic Accents by James D. Price, except here Kadma V'Azla is treated as one accent instead of two. Note that this does not apply to books of Psalms, Proverbs, and Job, which use a different system.

How to use this page: To find out what accent can come after a given accent, follow these rules for moving from cell to cell in the first table below:

  • Within a row, you can only move directly left, although you may optionally skip any conjunctive accents (those in parentheses).
  • Once you reach the end of the row, you may then jump to any cell in that column or a column to the right.
  • If an accent appears by itself – i.e. with nothing from its row directly before it – then it may need to be substituted (see the second table below).
  • An asterisk (*) means this accent only be used once per verse, as the first of its rank or higher before Sof-pasuk (these are Etnachta and Segol).

Colors: When hovering over the tables below:

  • Light red indicate all accents which could have come before the current accent (without jumping),
  • Orange indicates the current accent or one its substitutes,
  • Yellow indicates all accents which can come immediately after the current accent, and
  • Light green indicates all accents which could come further after the current accent (without jumping).

Note: Conjunctive accents (those in parentheses) have less clear-cut rules defining their usage. For example, many of them do not have a just one accent that they always pair with – duplicates in the table below are marked in light gray. Additionally, more than one such accent can appear in a row. Aside from the two cases account for below where a regular accent is substituted for a conjunctive one, this additional accent can be a repeat of what comes after it, a Munach, a Mapach, a Kadma, or a Darga (from most to least common).

Understanding the System of Hebrew Accents

Analogy: In a long verse, the accents at the ends of their respective rows have the following approximate correspondences in English punctuation:

  • The Emperors Sof-pasuk and Etnachta correspond to a period and a semicolon or dash, respectively.
  • The Kings Segol and Zakef correspond to dashes or commas.
  • The Duke R'via corresponds to a comma or an unmarked boundary between two phrases.
  • The Count Pazer corresponds to a unmarked boundary between two phrases.
  • The conjuctive accents (those in parentheses) are used to mark words within a phrase.
In shorter verses, this correspondence often shifts up, e.g. with Etnachta corresponding to a comma or even an unmarked boundary in an extremely short verse.
